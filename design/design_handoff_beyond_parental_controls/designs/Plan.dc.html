Graduated Internet Access Plan

The full, candid plan. A kid-facing version lives on the kid site, safe to hand over.

For: an Apple household, your kid at age 9. Younger siblings age into each stage. Goal: locked-down → full autonomy by 18, on a deliberate schedule instead of by attrition. Built from: ~70 sources — peer-reviewed research, NCMEC/FBI data, and AAP/APA guidance (see the research).

Section 1

What the evidence actually says

  1. 1Filters barely work as a shield — but that's not why you deploy them. The best study found filtering explains <0.5% of the variance in whether a teen encounters sexual material. They still earn their keep in elementary school: they cut accidental exposure and buy years to teach.
  2. 2Talking beats restricting — for harm. A 52-study meta-analysis (N=74,159): restrictive rules cut screen time; active mediation cuts harm. Tools as a supplement, conversation as the core.
  3. 3Rules have an expiration date: ~12 they protect, ~16 they backfire. Your step-down schedule is not generosity — it's the mechanism.
  4. 4The disclosure channel is your single most valuable asset. 40% of kids said they'd tell you if an adult sent them nudes; only 10% of those it happened to did. The evidence-backed move is the pledge, said out loud and honored:
    "You will never be in trouble for telling me something that happened online, and I will never respond by taking your devices away. Even if you broke a rule first."
Section 2

The stack — set up once, before school starts

No single layer survives a motivated 13-year-old, so you run several thin layers and let the redundancy work. Estimated setup: one weekend.

LAYER 0 · IDENTITY
A supervised child Apple Account in Family Sharing per kid, true birthdate. Your email as recovery on every account.
LAYER 1 · NETWORK
A filtering DNS resolver (e.g. NextDNS), one profile per kid. Router points DHCP at it and enforces a hard bedtime cutoff — the single most evidence-backed control here.
LAYER 2 · APPLE (SCREEN TIME)
Web Content → Allowed Websites Only for grade 4. Communication Limits + Communication Safety on. Ask to Buy. Treat Screen Time as policy signal, not enforcement — the network layer is the backstop.
LAYER 5 · MONITORING
The honest default: skip the monitoring apps. Use DNS logs + platform-native family tools + weekly sit-downs. Covert monitoring backfires.
Section 3 · The centerpiece

The year-by-year schedule

Each stage lists what unlocks, what stays locked, and a gate — observable behaviors that green-light the next. The grade labels are one fixed schedule; the Balanced pace just moves where your kid sits on it. Right now: your kid is at Grade 4, Grade 5 up next.

9

Grade 4 · age 9 — "Finding your balance, together"

You are here

The year of supervised everything. Goal: build the tell-an-adult reflex before independence, and bank hundreds of boring-normal internet hours with you nearby.

Unlocks
  • Browsing on allowlist, in common rooms
  • YouTube Kids "Older" preset, co-viewed
  • Minecraft, chat off, single-player / local co-op
Stays locked
  • Open search; any algorithmic feed
  • YouTube main app; Shorts
  • All chat/DMs; Discord
  • Devices in bedrooms, ever
Teach this year: the exit plan for disturbing content — close it → come tell me → you are not in trouble. And the first porn conversation happens THIS YEAR: calm, brief, no shame.

Gate to the next stage: your kid closes-and-tells on something weird at least once; can explain why a stranger offering free Robux wants something; keeps passwords from friends.

10

Grade 5 · age 10 — "Doing more on your own"

Solo-but-visible. Real searching and research skills, with the filter catching the potholes.

Unlocks: category filter + SafeSearch-enforced search · in-game chat with in-person friends · maybe a basic phone.  Locked: algorithmic feeds · DMs · Discord · smartphone.
11

Grade 6 · age 11 — "Talking to real people"

Private communication with known humans, still zero engagement-optimized feeds — the plan's core bet.

Unlocks: personal email · iMessage with approved contacts · group chats with known kids.  Locked: social media of any kind · Snapchat/TikTok/Instagram · short-form feeds.
12

Grade 7 · age 12 — "Leveling up your skills"

The full drill. Heavy on offense (skills) while access barely moves — because skills unlock access.

Unlocks: multiplayer voice with in-person friends · more supervised YouTube.  Locked: open-lobby voice · TikTok/IG/Snap · AI companion apps.
13

Grade 8 · age 13 — "Your first accounts"

The year of 13 — deliberately, not automatically. 13 is a COPPA artifact, not a readiness certificate.

Unlocks: smartphone by end of 8th · one social platform, teen account, supervision linked.  Locked: the other platforms · 16+/18+ tiers · AI companions.
14

Grade 9 · age 14 — "Trust, with check-ins"

Controls start visibly falling away. They should feel the difference on day one of high school.

Unlocks: 2nd/3rd platform · Discord normalized · App Store 16+ tier.  Steps down: web filter narrows to porn/malware only · app limits → their self-set limits.
15

Grade 10 · age 15 — "We mostly step back"

The year you mostly get out of the way. Ride the step-down to the line; don't hold past it.

Unlocks: effectively open browsing · reverse-mentoring a younger sibling.  Steps down: all app time limits · content-tier caps below 18+ · log visibility → mutual dashboard.
16

Grade 11 · age 16 — "We're your advisors now"

Essentially open internet. DNS narrows to security-only. No monitoring. What remains: bedtime router rule if sleep demands it, family tech-talk culture, and the standing offer.

18

Grade 12 · The handoff — full autonomy

Full access, zero controls, a formal exit interview (make it dinner, not a meeting). They own every account, recovery method, and 2FA key; credit frozen; they know Take It Down, CyberTipline, and 988 exist — for their friends as much as themselves.

"The filters were never the point. You were the security system we were building. Call me before you call anyone when something goes wrong — that part never expires."
If the worst happens — the 60-minute playbook

Sextortion in progress: Believe them. Say the pledge back. Do NOT pay, do NOT delete the thread. Screenshot everything. Block. Report to the CyberTipline + FBI. Submit images to takeitdown.ncmec.org (works on AI fakes; the image never leaves the device). The threat usually collapses within days once they stop engaging.
