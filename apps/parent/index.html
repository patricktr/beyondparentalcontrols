Beyond Parental Controls

Filters and screen-time limits work when kids are little — and quietly backfire right when the stakes get highest. The fix isn't more control. It's handing autonomy over on purpose, on a schedule you can both see — instead of losing it by attrition.

What the research actually says

  • Filters barely work as a shield. They explain under 0.5% of whether a teen encounters explicit material. But they do cut accidental exposure for young kids and buy you years to teach — so use them there, and never mistake them for the strategy.
  • Rules protect kids under ~12 and backfire on teens over ~16. Strict controls predict less problematic use in little kids and more in teenagers. The step-down isn't generosity — it's the mechanism.
  • Talking beats restricting — for harm. Restricting cuts screen time; coaching and co-using cut actual harm. This plan front-loads the skills and the conversations, then removes the guardrails on a published schedule.
